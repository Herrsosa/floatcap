The Hidden Cost of Marketplace Distribution for AI Startups

Selling through Microsoft, AWS, or Salesforce marketplaces is one of the fastest paths to enterprise revenue for an AI company. What nobody talks about is what happens to your cash flow once the deal closes.

Selling through Microsoft, AWS, or Salesforce marketplaces is one of the fastest paths to enterprise revenue for an AI company. The buyer already has budget allocated. Procurement is simplified. Trust is inherited from the platform. For many startups, marketplace distribution is the difference between a six-month sales cycle and a six-week one.

What nobody talks about is what happens to your cash flow once the deal closes.

How marketplace payouts actually work

Cloud marketplaces are not payment processors. They are enterprise procurement platforms that happen to collect money on your behalf. The distinction matters because the payout mechanics are built for the convenience of the buyer and the platform, not the seller.

Here is how the three major marketplaces handle your money.

Microsoft Marketplace (via Partner Center)

Microsoft collects payment from the enterprise buyer. Your earnings are calculated at the end of each calendar month. Payouts are initiated around the 15th of the following month, but only if your account has met the minimum payout threshold. From there, the transfer to your bank account takes 3 to 10 business days depending on your payment method and region.

In practice, this means revenue earned in the first week of March might not hit your bank account until late April. Revenue earned in the last week of March might not arrive until early May. The effective delay from the point of sale to cash in your account ranges from 45 to 75 days.

AWS Marketplace

AWS operates on a similar monthly cycle but adds a layer of complexity through its disbursement process. Seller revenue is calculated after the end of the billing period, and disbursements are processed after a holding period that typically runs 30 to 60 days from the billing date.

For usage-based pricing (which is the dominant model for AI products), each month's usage is billed, collected, and disbursed on its own cycle. A customer who consumed $50,000 in inference-powered features in February might generate a disbursement that arrives in your account in April or May.

Salesforce AppExchange

Salesforce takes a revenue share and operates on its own billing and remittance cycle. For managed package applications, Salesforce handles billing through its platform and remits your share after the customer has paid and the applicable hold period has elapsed.

The Salesforce cycle is generally slower than Microsoft or AWS. Some ISV partners report payout timelines of 60 to 90 days from the point of customer usage.

The compounding effect on AI companies

For a traditional SaaS company, marketplace payout delays are an inconvenience. Your hosting costs are relatively fixed and modest. A two-month delay in receiving $100,000 is annoying but survivable if your marginal cost of serving that customer is $10,000.

For an AI company, the same delay is structurally dangerous. Here is a worked example.

Suppose you run an AI document processing tool sold through the Microsoft Marketplace. You price at $0.05 per page processed. In March, a new enterprise customer onboards and processes 2 million pages. That is $100,000 in revenue.

To serve those 2 million pages, your application made roughly 500,000 API calls to an LLM provider. Your inference bill for March is $40,000. Your LLM provider invoices you at the end of March, payment due within 30 days.

Your unit economics ($100k revenue vs $55k total costs) are healthy. But Microsoft will not pay you until early May. Your LLM provider wants their $40k by the end of April. You have $100k in receivables and $55k in immediate obligations, and the cash is sitting in Microsoft’s treasury.

Multiply this by five customers, and your monthly obligations hit $275,000 — all due 30 to 60 days before the corresponding revenue arrives.

The metrics that matter (and the ones that don't)

Traditional lenders evaluate businesses on gross margin, EBITDA, and debt-to-equity ratios. For AI companies selling through marketplaces, these metrics either mislead or miss the point entirely.

Gross margin is misleading because inference costs are variable, usage-dependent, and improving over time. Rejecting a loan application based on a margin snapshot is like rejecting a manufacturing loan because raw material prices were temporarily elevated.

EBITDA is misleading because it does not capture the timing of cash flows. A company can be EBITDA-positive on a quarterly basis while being cash-flow-negative on a monthly basis purely because of payout timing.

The metrics that actually matter for underwriting an AI company include Revenue per dollar of inference, Payout cycle duration, and Inference cost trajectory.

What founders can do today

If you are an AI founder running into this cash flow timing problem, there are a few practical steps worth considering while the lending market catches up:

  • Negotiate compute terms. Committed-use discounts often come with more favourable billing terms.
  • Structure marketplace contracts carefully. Push for annual upfront billing where possible.
  • Track your real working capital gap. Build a model that accounts for your exact payout timing.
  • Separate your unit economics from blended financials. Show your profitability at the inference level.
